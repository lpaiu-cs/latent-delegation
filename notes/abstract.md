We study one-way latent delegation between same-family open models in a constrained single-GPU setting. The default system uses Gemma-2 9B as the large model and Gemma-2 2B as the delegated small model. The large model retains ownership of the master residual stream and final logits, while a removed middle block is replaced by an entry projector, a frozen delegated small-model window, a return adapter, and a learned scalar gate. All backbone weights remain frozen, and training is limited to lightweight interface modules. On a native Windows RTX 5090-class machine, the same-family Gemma path runs successfully in 4-bit and the hybrid forward path clears a conservative `seq_len=256` smoke matrix. Stage A representation alignment is stable, and hidden-only Stage B improves hidden recovery over skip-only and a no-small passthrough control. Adding teacher-logit supervision directly to Stage B produces the main positive result: the hybrid improves over both `skip_only` and `hybrid_no_small` at the output level on held-out text probes. However, the hybrid does not outperform strong large-space bridge controls, including a parameter-matched bridge baseline. A final follow-up that allows the entry projector to keep adapting during output-aware Stage B improves hidden metrics but does not improve the hybrid's output KL or NLL and widens the gap to the bridge controls. The resulting conclusion is qualified: same-family latent delegation is feasible and functionally useful relative to skip/no-small controls, but this work does not show that delegated small-model computation is superior to strong bridge-based alternatives.
