# Introduction

This project asks a narrow feasibility question rather than a broad model-improvement question: can a large same-family model keep the master hidden state and final logits while delegating part of its middle computation to a smaller same-family model through latent-space transfer? The framing matters. This is not a claim of full thought transfer, model equivalence, or general downstream superiority. It is a controlled test of whether delegated latent computation can recover useful work under severe practical constraints.

Those constraints are deliberate. The target setting is a single RTX 5090-class GPU, same-family open models only, frozen backbones, and lightweight learned interface modules. The default pair is Gemma-2 9B and Gemma-2 2B. That makes the project interpretable: if the hybrid fails, it fails inside a constrained but realistic proof-of-concept regime; if it succeeds, the success can be traced to a specific latent substitution mechanism rather than to large-scale finetuning or distributed systems engineering.

The early repo state, frozen as `v0.5.1`, already established a qualified feasibility result. Delegated small-model computation clearly helped relative to `skip_only` and no-small controls, including after an output-aware Stage B objective was introduced. But the hybrid still did not beat the strongest bridge baselines that stayed entirely in large hidden space. That left an unresolved structural question: was the delegated idea itself weak, or was the original fixed contiguous substitution window simply the wrong prior?

The continuation work answered that question positively enough to matter. Real Gemma Phase 1 rejected the old fixed `24..29 -> 14..19` split as the best default and produced a near-tied shortlist of asymmetric windows. A static two-path mixture over that shortlist was the first pilot result to beat both bridge controls, and a minimal token-wise two-path gate improved further. That token-wise model became the frozen `v0.6.0` best result.

The paper therefore has two jobs. First, it must explain the bounded positive result clearly: a token-wise two-path delegated hybrid beats strong bridge controls on the repo’s main held-out LM-style probes, and that win survives a fresh untouched holdout. Second, it must keep the claim boundary disciplined: broader external generalization exists but is mixed, later Idea 5 and Idea 2 branches are scientific analysis rather than new best models, and the evidence does not support a broad “delegation beats bridges everywhere” story.
